Soft Ground

Nothing about youhas gone wrong

Anxiety this bad is frightening and exhausting. It is also very common, and every part of it has a clear explanation.

This app does two things. It explains what anxiety is doing to your body and your thoughts. Then it gives you practical steps to make it smaller.

Nothing here asks you to do more than you can manage today. Nothing you type is saved or sent anywhere. If the screen is too bright, too dark, or the text is too small, use the coloured circles in the top corner to change it.

Where would you like to start?
One

Your body is not damaged

The pounding heart, the shaking and the sick feeling are not signs of damage. They are not warnings that something worse is about to happen. Your body is preparing you to escape from danger at a moment when there is no danger.

Two

The fear was learned

Anxiety this strong is not something you were born with. It was built up in stages, from things that happened to you and the conclusions you drew from them. Anything learned in stages can be unlearned in stages.

Three

There is a way out

Not a quick trick, and not overnight. There is a clear order to it: settle your body, work out what you are actually afraid of, then take small steps back into normal life. It is slow, and it works.

Why being told to calm down never worked

People have probably told you to calm down, to push through it, or to stop overthinking. When that did not work, you may have decided that the problem is you.

It is not. Those instructions failed because they were aimed at the wrong part of your brain. The part that creates fear does not respond to reasoning or advice. It learns from experience only.

This means it has to be shown, over and over, that the thing you are afraid of does not actually harm you. It will not accept being told.

That is what this app is for. Not to argue you out of the fear, but to show that part of your brain something it will actually learn from.

Your body

What anxiety does
to your body

Every symptom you get has a purpose. Your body is preparing you to run away from danger. Once you know what each sensation is for, most of them stop being frightening, because most of the fear comes from not knowing what they are.

Your brain reacted before you could think

One part of your brain has a single job: watch for danger. It reacts before you have finished having a thought about it. It works that fast because the dangers it was designed for could kill a person in seconds.

It is fast, but it is not accurate. It cannot tell the difference between a lecture theatre and a real threat. It would rather set off a false alarm a hundred times than miss a real one once.

So it sets off the alarm. Your body then does everything it would do if you had to run for your life. You did not choose any of this and you cannot argue with it, because it started before the thinking part of your brain had caught up.

You are not overreacting. Your body is doing exactly what it was built to do, in a situation where it is not needed. Those are two different things, and neither of them is your fault.

What each symptom is for

Read whichever ones happen to you. Much of the fear in anxiety comes from not knowing what these sensations are.

Your heart is pounding

Your heart is pumping blood to your arms and legs, because your body expects you to run. Your heart is not failing. It is doing something healthy, and it can beat this fast for a long time without harming you.

You cannot get a full breath

You are getting plenty of air. You are actually breathing slightly too much, because your body is breathing for a run that never happens. This is what causes the tingling, the light head and the strange floating feeling. It is uncomfortable and it is harmless. It settles as soon as your breathing slows down.

You are shaking

Your muscles are tensed and ready to move. They have been told to run, and no run has happened. The shaking is that unused energy. Walking around, or shaking your hands out on purpose, uses it up faster than sitting still.

Your stomach has dropped

Digesting food takes a lot of energy, so your body switches it off first. The blood that was doing that job goes to your arms and legs instead. That is the dropping feeling. It is also why you feel sick and why you might need the toilet.

You cannot think or remember anything

Your attention has been switched to watching for danger. You are not losing your mind and you have not become stupid. Your brain has borrowed the resources it normally uses for thinking, and it gives them back every time, once the alarm settles.

You are hot, sweating or blushing

Your body is cooling itself down before the exercise it expects. This is a hard one, because you can see it happening. It is worth knowing that blushing and sweating are almost always far less visible to other people than they feel to you.

Nothing feels real

This frightens people more than any other symptom, so here it is plainly: it is not madness and it is not the start of madness. It is a normal effect of high adrenaline and fast breathing. It is also the symptom people are most embarrassed to mention. It passes.

You are exhausted for hours afterwards

The first wave of chemicals is fast. A second, slower one is still moving through your body long after the moment has passed. That is why two minutes of panic can leave you tired for a whole afternoon. You are not being weak.

The most important part of this page

The fear rises, then it falls

It does this every time. Your body cannot keep the alarm at full strength, because it uses too much energy. The fear goes up, reaches a peak, and comes back down. It does this whether or not you do anything clever.

It has never once gone up and stayed up. Not in your whole life.

This matters, because the belief that traps most people is this one: if I do not escape, it will keep rising until something terrible happens. It will not. There is a limit. You have reached that limit many times already, and every single time, you came back down.

You do not have to believe this yet. Just keep it somewhere you can find it, because there will be a moment when you need it.

The cycle that keeps it going

You notice something in your body. Your chest goes tight, or your heart skips a beat. Then you think: something is wrong.

That thought is itself a danger signal. So your body releases more adrenaline. That creates stronger physical sensations. Those sensations make the thought more convincing. Then it goes round again, getting stronger each time.

This is why anxiety can seem to come from nowhere and build within seconds. Often the first sensation was not fear at all. It might have been coffee, or standing up too quickly, or a bad night's sleep. Your body produced a sensation, and your mind read it as a warning.

This gives you a useful place to step in. You cannot decide to stop being afraid. But you can learn to stop treating a physical sensation as proof that something is wrong. That is a smaller job, and you can get better at it with practice.

If you need it right now

Three things that help in the moment

Breathe out slowly. Not deep breaths. Long ones. Make the out-breath longer than the in-breath. Your body treats a slow out-breath as a sign that the danger has passed, because you cannot breathe out slowly while you are being chased.

Say out loud what you are feeling. For example: my heart is racing, I feel sick, I am frightened. Putting feelings into words settles them in a way that thinking about them does not. It seems far too simple to work. Do it anyway.

Move. Walk to the end of the corridor and back. Your body has prepared you to move and is confused that you have not. Give it two minutes of movement.

The basics

Four things that make everything else easier

None of the harder work is possible if your body is running on empty. People skip this part because it sounds too obvious to matter.

Sleep. A tired brain is a more frightened brain, every time.

Food. When your blood sugar drops you get shaky and your heart speeds up. Those feelings are physically identical to panic, and your mind will read them as panic.

Caffeine. Coffee and energy drinks produce the exact sensations you are most afraid of. If you drink a lot, cutting down for two weeks is worth trying on its own.

Movement. Regular exercise, hard enough to make you breathe faster, does more for anxiety than almost anything else you can do without a prescription.

This is not about being disciplined. You are trying to do something difficult, and it is much harder when your body has nothing left. Sort this part out first.

How it was built

The fear was built
in five steps

Anxiety does not feel like something you learned. It feels like a plain fact about the world. But it was built up in stages, and each stage was added by experience. Once you can see the stages, you can start undoing them.

The five steps

Read these slowly. Most people recognise themselves at about the third one.

OneSomething happened

Usually something small, and usually a long time ago. Someone laughed at the wrong moment. A teacher looked at you a certain way. You were asked to read aloud. There was a pause after you spoke that felt too long. It may have been so minor that nobody else remembers it, including the people involved.

TwoYou decided what it meant

Then you did what everyone does when they are young. You decided what it meant about you, not about the situation. Something like: I am the sort of person people laugh at. Or: there is something wrong with me and people can see it. You were far too young to be making that judgement. You made it in about a second, and you have never gone back to check whether it was true.

ThreeYou made rules to stay safe

Once you believed that, you made rules. Never speak first. Always know exactly what you are going to say. Never stand out. Get it right or do not do it at all. You never wrote these down. You just started living by them, until you stopped noticing they were choices.

FourYou started predicting disasters

Rules like that make you predict what will go wrong. You walk towards a room and you do not see the room. You see what you think is about to happen in it, in detail, with your face already burning. Your mind shows you a disaster in advance and presents it to you as information.

FiveYou stopped finding out

After enough of those predictions, you started avoiding the situations. This is the part that keeps it going. Because you avoided them, you never got to find out whether the predictions were right. So they were never corrected, and they still feel true today.

Where you can change it

You cannot change step one, and you do not need to

Whatever happened, happened. Nothing can change that. Be careful of anyone who tells you the answer is to dig up more of your past. Plenty of people never work out what their original event was, and they still get better.

The step that matters most is the second one: the meaning you gave it.

That meaning was never a fact. It was a conclusion, reached by a child, very fast, with almost no information, and never reviewed since.

Conclusions can be changed. Not by arguing with them, but by collecting real experiences that do not match them. That is what the small steps in the plan are for. They look like they are about doing frightening things. They are really about gathering proof that an old conclusion about you was wrong.

The first thing keeping it going

The critical voice

It is trying to protect you

The voice in your head is constant, specific and cruel. It says things to you that you would never say to another person. It uses your own voice, which is why you have never thought to question it.

Here is what it is actually doing. It learned that being humiliated is unbearable. So it worked out that if it says the worst thing first, nobody else can surprise you with it. It criticises you in advance so that criticism from other people cannot land as a shock.

It believes this is helping you. It is like a bodyguard who has become so worried about your safety that he now shouts at you himself, to prove he is still needed.

Why it sounds so certain

Because it is never tested. It makes its judgements, such as they think you are strange or everyone can tell. Then you avoid the situation, so the judgement is never checked against what actually happens.

Anything that is never tested gets more confident over time, not less. That is true of people and it is true of the voice in your head.

Notice the words it uses. Everyone. Always. Never. Obviously. Nobody who was honestly describing a room full of people would use the word everyone. That is a clue about how accurate it is being.

What to do about it

You do not have to win the argument

You have probably spent years trying to prove the voice wrong. Gathering evidence. Reassuring yourself. Asking other people to reassure you. It works for about ten minutes, and then it starts again.

That happens because you cannot argue something into silence when it does not care about evidence. So stop arguing with it. There is a different move, and it is smaller.

Let it talk, and stop obeying it.

A thought can be very loud and very convincing and still only be a thought. Your mind produces thousands of thoughts an hour and you ignore most of them. You do not have to agree with this one. You do not have to disagree with it either. You only have to stop treating it as an instruction.

The first time you manage this it will feel like almost nothing. There will be a small gap between the thought and you. That gap is what you are building, and everything else you do from here gets built inside it.

The second thing keeping it going

Avoiding things

The relief is real

You decide not to go, and something in your chest loosens. You get a whole evening back. The dread stops. In that moment it is one of the best feelings there is, and anyone who pretends otherwise has never felt it.

Nobody is going to tell you off for that here. Wanting relief is not a character flaw.

But that relief teaches your brain something. It arrives fast and it feels strong, straight after the decision. So your brain records it as: that was dangerous, and not going is what saved me.

This is why the fear does not stay the same size. It grows. It also spreads. First the lecture, then the building, then the bus that goes past the building, then any afternoon when someone might message you. That is not you getting worse as a person. It is a very literal system being taught the same lesson repeatedly, and learning it well.

The small things you do to get through

These are the habits you use to get through. Practising a sentence nine times before you say it. Holding a cup so your hands do not shake visibly. Sitting near the door. Keeping your coat on. Saying very little. Only going if one particular person will be there. Arriving late so you miss the small talk.

They feel like the reason you got through it. Almost always, they are the reason you never found out that you would have got through it anyway.

Here is why. When it goes fine, the voice says: yes, but only because you held the cup. So nothing gets learned, and you have to bring the cup again next time. Then the cup becomes another thing you cannot manage without.

You do not have to stop all of them. That would be miserable and it is not what is being suggested. Stop one of them, once, and see what happens.

Have a look

Which of these do you do?

Tap any you recognise. This is not to make you feel bad. Everyone does these, including people you would never guess. You just cannot put something down until you have noticed you are carrying it.

What to do about it

Going is what helps. Feeling calm is not the aim.

Almost everyone waits until they feel ready. They wait for the fear to drop to a manageable level, and then they will go.

It does not work in that order, and waiting is how years go past.

The fear does not drop first. You go, the fear comes with you, nothing bad happens, and that is what makes the fear drop next time.

Feeling terrified while you do it does not mean you did it wrong. It means you did it at all.

So the aim is never to go and feel fine. The aim is to go, feel awful, come home, and notice that you are still here. Do that enough times and that part of your brain slowly starts to update.

The plan

What to do,
in order

There are four stages. Most people move back and forth between them rather than finishing one and starting the next. Having a bad week after a good one does not mean you have failed. It happens to everyone who does this. Start at stage one, even if you think you do not need it.

Stage oneSettle your body

The aim is not to feel calm. It is to be less worn out than you are now.

You cannot do frightening things when your body is exhausted, underfed and full of caffeine. This stage is dull, and it is the one that makes everything after it possible. Give it two weeks before you decide whether it is working.

  • Breathe out slowly for three minutes, twice a day. Do this when you are calm, not when you are panicking. You are learning the skill now so that you have it later.
  • Move your body most days. Ten minutes counts. Doing it badly counts.
  • Eat something before midday, even if you are not hungry.
  • Go to bed at roughly the same time each night. Roughly is fine.
  • Halve your caffeine for two weeks and see what changes.
  • Do one thing each week that has nothing to do with anxiety.
Stage twoWork out what you are actually afraid of

The aim is to turn one huge vague fear into a list of specific ones.

At the moment the anxiety probably feels enormous and impossible to describe. It is not one thing. It is a set of particular situations, particular predictions and particular habits. A fear you have named is always smaller than one you have not.

  • Once a day, catch one prediction and write it down in plain words. For example: I think I will go blank and everyone will notice.
  • Write down three of the small habits you use to get through. Just write them down. Do not stop doing them yet.
  • If you notice one of your rules, write that down too. For example: never speak first.
  • Notice the times when it is less bad. Those moments are useful information and almost nobody thinks to look at them.

Do not go looking for the original event from step one. If it comes to you, fine. If it does not, you lose nothing.

Stage threeTake small steps back

The aim is to collect proof. You are not trying to become braver.

Here is the whole method. Decide what you think will happen. Go and do it. Find out what actually happens. You are running a small test on an old belief.

  • Choose something very small. Then make it smaller. If it does not feel slightly silly, it is still too big.
  • Write your prediction down before you go. This matters, because afterwards your memory will quietly change it.
  • Go and do it. You will feel awful. Stay a little past the worst of it if you can.
  • Write down what actually happened, using plain words and no judgements about yourself.
  • Do the same step several times before moving to a harder one. Doing it once proves nothing. Doing it four times starts to prove something.
  • At some point, drop one of your small coping habits for one attempt, and see what it costs you.
Stage fourWiden it out

The aim is to get your life back, which is not the same as feeling calm.

Once things start moving, there is a risk of building a slightly bigger but still small life: one shop, one route, one safe friend. So you deliberately vary things.

  • Repeat a step you have already done, but change one thing about it. A different place, a different person or a different time of day.
  • Keep doing things while you still feel afraid. That was always the skill.
  • Let bad days be bad days. A hard week does not mean the whole thing has stopped working, even though it will feel like that at the time.
  • Start choosing things because you want to do them, not because they are practice. That is what recovery actually feels like.
For today only

What are you doing today?

Choose the stage you are on. Tick things off as you do them. Nothing is saved. This is only here to hold today's plan so you do not have to keep it in your head.

The tool for stage three

Your steps

Write one step per line. Be exact. Not "talk to people", but "ask the man in the corner shop for a bag". Write down what you think will happen. Then come back afterwards and write down what actually happened. The difference between those two is how you get better.

You have not added any steps yet. They sort themselves with the easiest first, so add whatever comes to mind. Nothing is saved, so print the list if you want to keep it.

Three things nobody tells you about doing this

It gets slightly worse before it gets better. You have spent a long time avoiding things, so facing them is genuinely harder in the first week than staying home was. This does not mean it is not working. It is the point where most people give up.

Progress is not steady, and you will not feel it. You will have an easy week and decide you are better. Then you will have a Thursday that feels like the first day again. Both are normal. Judge this over months, and judge it by what you are doing, not by how you feel.

You will want to do it perfectly. That urge comes from the same place as the anxiety. Doing this badly and irregularly still works. That is how most people who get better actually do it.

You do not have to do this alone

Everything here can be done on your own, and doing it on your own is a real option. But if anxiety has taken your studies, your work, or your ability to leave the house, please tell a doctor as well.

That is not admitting failure. It is a sensible thing to do about a difficult problem. A GP can also write you a Mental Health Treatment Plan, which makes seeing a psychologist much cheaper.

Exercises

Small things you can
actually do

You do not need to do these every day, or in order, or all of them. Pick one and do it once, even badly. That is not a weaker version of the real thing. It is the thing. Leave the others until you want them.

Two to five minutes

Slow breathing

Follow the ring. Breathe in through your nose as it grows. Hold while it stays still. Breathe out through your mouth as it shrinks, slowly, as if through a straw. Then rest for a moment before the next one. The out-breath is longer than the in-breath on purpose, because that is the part your body reads as safety. If you feel light-headed, you are breathing in too hard. Take smaller breaths and slow the out-breath down.

 
Ready

Slower is not automatically better. Choose the speed you can keep up without effort. If you are straining, it is not working.

The ring stays still while you hold, and a line moves round it so you can see the time passing. If holding your breath makes you feel short of air, choose None. That reaction is common and it is not a failure.

One minute

Name what you are feeling

Choose up to three words that are true right now. Being specific helps more than being dramatic. "Exposed" and "dread" do more than "bad". Then say the sentence out loud, even quietly. It seems too simple to do anything. It does do something.

Notice that you have described a feeling, not made a statement about who you are. "I feel small" and "I am small" are different sentences, and only the first one is true.

Two minutes

Say it as if you were talking to someone else

Write the thought exactly as it arrives in your head. Then read the version underneath out loud. Hearing it in the third person makes it easier to be fair, in the same way you are fair about other people's problems but not your own.

One minute

Step back from the thought

Type whatever is going round in your head. Four versions of it will appear. Read them in order, slowly. You are not trying to believe or disbelieve any of them. You are practising standing slightly outside the thought instead of inside it.

Three minutes

Would you say it to a friend?

When you are anxious you say things to yourself that you would never say to another person. This is a quick way to see how unfair it has become. Check whether the standard you are holding yourself to is one you would apply to anybody else.

Ninety seconds

Put your attention on the room

When you are frightened, your attention turns inwards and starts checking on you: your voice, your face, how you are coming across. Then you use that as evidence for how you seem to others, which is not fair evidence. This is practice at putting your attention back on the room. Do it with your eyes open, in the actual situation.

Five minutes

What matters to you

Anxiety only ever asks one question: is this safe? A life organised entirely around that question gets very small. This asks a different one: what do you want your life to be about? Choose no more than three.

Reflections

Something to read
and repeat quietly

Twelve short readings, one for each of the things anxiety wears down. Read one slowly. At the end of each there are two or three lines you can keep and say to yourself during the day, silently, whenever the old thought turns up.

Today

This changes each day. You do not have to read them in order, and you can pick a different one from the list below whenever you want.

How to use these

Read the whole thing once, slowly. It takes about a minute.

Then look at the lines at the end. Choose the one that is easiest to believe today, not the one that sounds best. Say it to yourself a few times, silently, and again later when the old thought comes back.

These are not affirmations and they are deliberately not written as ones. Telling yourself things you do not believe tends to make you feel worse, not better, so nothing here asks you to say that you are confident or fearless. Every line is written to be something you could actually agree with today, even on a bad day.

If a line does not feel true, do not use it. Pick another, or leave the whole reading and come back to it another time.
